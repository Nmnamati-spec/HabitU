# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Students and young adults (roughly 16–28) building daily routines and holding themselves accountable. They open the app in the morning to log a habit, mid-day to check in on a streak, or to scroll the feed for motivation when willpower is low. They are social but not performative — they want encouragement from real peers, not a leaderboard.

## Product Purpose

HabitU helps students and young adults build lasting habits through a combination of personal tracking and social accountability. Success looks like users coming back daily not out of anxiety, but because the app feels like a warm place where progress is visible, celebrated, and shared.

## Brand Personality

Warm. Earned. Alive.

The energy is: you finished a morning run, you're glowing, you open an app that feels as good as that moment. Not corporate wellness. Not hustle-culture pressure. Real people, real progress, real community.

## References

- **Pinterest** — masonry feed, image-forward discovery, warm editorial palette, content that makes you want to scroll without feeling manipulated
- **Strava** — achievement culture done right, social proof without vanity metrics, activity cards with personality, athletic but human

What works about both: they have a *visual signature*. You know a Pinterest screenshot. You know a Strava activity card. HabitU should have that same recognition — a look that is its own thing.

## Anti-references

- Generic Material Design defaults — standard card grids, identical row items, no visual hierarchy
- Neon fitness apps (too aggressive, wrong emotional register)
- Corporate wellness (Calm, Headspace palette — too clinical, too soft)
- Gamified to the point of feeling childish (no progress bars that look like Duolingo)
- Anything that looks like it could have been generated by an AI in 30 seconds

## Design Principles

1. **Visual signature over visual safety.** Every screen should be recognizable as HabitU. Avoid default patterns — use opinionated layout, spacing, and color choices that build a consistent identity.
2. **Warmth is structural, not decorative.** Warmth comes from image-forward layouts, generous whitespace, and editorial typography — not from rounded corners and pastel gradients.
3. **Motion earns its place.** Every animation reinforces the habit loop: logging a habit should feel satisfying, not just functional. Use motion to celebrate, guide, and reward — never for decoration.
4. **Achievement without anxiety.** Show progress in ways that feel earned and honest. No streak-guilt mechanics. Milestones should feel like a fist-bump from a friend, not a notification you're afraid to dismiss.
5. **The feed is a mood board, not a timeline.** The social feed should feel like discovery — varied card sizes, rich imagery, editorial hierarchy. Not a flat list of identical posts.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Target WCAG AA contrast ratios throughout
- Support reduced motion for users with vestibular sensitivities (respect system `prefers-reduced-motion` equivalent on Android)
- Ensure core flows work with TalkBack (content descriptions on all interactive elements and images)
- Color is never the sole indicator of state (use shape/icon/label alongside color)
